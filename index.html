Your 2 Screens — What to Design

Detailed spec for each screen: what it shows, what the user does, and exactly what UI elements to include.

Screen 01 · 12:00 PM Use Case

Smart Order — Morning Order Review

Radhika opens Roz after breakfast. In 90 seconds she reviews today's AI-generated order for fresh produce, adjusts one item, and sends it to the DC. Done. The old process took 60 minutes.

  • Header: "Aaj ka order" + store name + date. Simple orange nav bar.
  • Summary bar: Total items · Total qty · Estimated ₹ value · Risk flag (e.g. "3 items low confidence")
  • Order list: Each item = name in Hindi + English, AI qty rec, current stock, yesterday sold, trend arrow (↑↓), confidence pill (High/Med/Low). Stepper to adjust qty.
  • Confidence explained: Low confidence items expand to show why ("festival tomorrow — uncertain demand")
  • Action bar (sticky bottom): "Approve All" (primary orange CTA) + "Save draft" secondary
  • Status strip: "Last order sent 2 days ago — wastage 4.2 kg" — feedback loop visible
Screen 02 · 8:00 PM Use Case

Daily Briefing — Auto-Generated HQ Report

Radhika opens Roz at store close. Her daily HQ report is already written — pulled from POS and inventory. She reads it, adds one voice note, taps send. 2 minutes. Not 60.

  • Header: "Aaj ka report" + auto-generated timestamp. "Ready to send" status chip.
  • KPI row (3 numbers): Today's sales vs target · Wastage kg · In-stock % — color coded green/red
  • Anomaly callouts: 1–3 flagged items ("Tomato sales 40% below yesterday — possible quality issue"). Each has a "Mark resolved" tap.
  • Summary text: 3-line auto-written summary in Hindi. Editable. Manager can tap to add voice note.
  • Expiry cleared: List of items marked down today + qty recovered vs written off
  • Action bar (sticky bottom): "Send to Area Manager" (WhatsApp share) + "Edit" secondary

SECONDARY USER
Vikram Nair
Area Manager · 8 stores
41 years old · Pune region · 14 yrs retail
"I visit 2 stores a day. By the time I reach a store something has already gone wrong."
His World

Vikram oversees 8 stores across Pune. He spends his day driving between stores, doing audits against a standardised checklist — sales vs target, front-of-house execution, stockroom, cash, people. He's measured on aggregate store performance: wastage, in-stock, sales, compliance scores.

His biggest problem: he can't trust the data Radhika sends him. It's manually compiled, inconsistently formatted, and sometimes wrong. He makes strategic decisions (which store to visit, where to escalate) based on information that's 12–18 hours old and error-prone.

Goals
📊
See accurate, real-time data across all 8 stores without visiting
🚨
Know which store needs attention before he drives there
📋
Audit reports that write themselves — consistent, photo-backed
Frustrations
🔴 HIGH
Data reliability. "Scoring drift" — he can't compare Radhika's store to another manager's store because each one reports differently. 15–20 point variance between equivalent stores on the same metric.
🔴 HIGH
Reactive visits. He drives 45 min to a store because yesterday's report looked bad. Half the time the issue is already resolved — or was reported wrong.
🟡 MED
Post-audit reporting. After each store visit he spends 30–40 min typing up findings, photos, corrective actions. All manual.
💡
Design implication: Roz's auto-generated daily report is as valuable to Vikram as to Radhika. Consistent format, same data source, anomaly-flagged = he can manage 8 stores from his car instead of reacting to bad data.

Frustrations
🟡 MED
No task visibility. He doesn't know which items are near-expiry unless Radhika tells him. She's often too busy. Items expire quietly.
🟡 MED
Reactive restocking. Customer asks for something → he checks → empty shelf → tells Radhika → she chases DC. 30 min wasted per incident.

What the personas tell us about Roz
01
Design for Radhika, not for Vikram
Radhika is the primary user. Every interaction must work on a mid-range Android, take under 2 minutes, and feel like WhatsApp. Vikram benefits passively through better data — he doesn't need a separate interface in V1.
02
Trust is earned through accuracy, not features
Radhika has 8 years of gut-feel expertise. If Roz's first order recommendation is wrong, she'll never use it again. The AI must explain its reasoning ("Based on last Tuesday's sales + tomorrow's local holiday") so she can verify it matches her instinct.
03
She must stay in control
Radhika doesn't want an AI that orders for her — she wants one that does the research and hands her a recommendation to approve. The approve/adjust model is critical. "AI suggests, I decide" is the mental model that will get adoption.
04
Hindi first, features second
Radhika thinks in Marathi, switches to Hindi, uses English numbers. The interface must lead in Hindi (or her regional language) with English numbers/units. Voice input is a natural extension — she's already using voice messages on WhatsApp.

UC-01
P1 · Ship
→ Screen 1
AI-Generated Fresh Produce Order
The highest-ROI decision in the store, replaced from gut feel to 1-tap approval
Trigger
Every day at 11:30 AM. Roz pulls previous 30-day sales by SKU from POS, checks current stock from inventory system, flags tomorrow's local holiday or weather anomaly, computes quantile order quantities (p40 conservative / p50 expected / p60 buffer).
What Radhika Sees
A push notification: "आज का ऑर्डर तैयार है — 24 items, ₹18,400". She taps → sees the order list sorted by confidence. Each item shows: name, recommended qty, current stock, yesterday's sales, trend arrow. Low-confidence items are flagged with the reason.
Her Action
Reviews in 2 minutes. Adjusts 1–2 items based on local knowledge (e.g. she knows a nearby wedding will spike onion demand). Taps "Approve" — order pushes directly to Oracle ERP indent flow.
Outcome
Order placed in 2 min vs 60 min. Forecast accuracy 76% vs 24%. Fresh wastage down 20–30%. Gross profit impact: +25% (More Retail's own validated result).
Edge cases handled
📵 Offline: order recommendation cached locally, submits when connectivity returns
✏️ Override: Radhika can adjust any qty — her change is logged and fed back to improve the model
⚠️ ERP down: shows "ERP unavailable — save draft and submit later" with 1-tap retry
🗓 Festival detected: banner says "Diwali in 3 days — order quantities increased 20% automatically. Adjust?"

UC-03
P1 · Ship
→ Alert layer
Near-Expiry Alert + Markdown Recommendation
Radhika used to find expiring items on her afternoon walk — too late for effective markdown
Trigger
Every morning at 8:00 AM, Roz scans the inventory batch table for items with expiry within 24 hours. It cross-references current stock qty and average hourly sales velocity to estimate if the item will sell out naturally or needs a markdown.
What Radhika Sees
A card on the Roz home screen: "⚠ 6 items expiring today". Tapping expands the list — each item shows: name, qty, expiry time, AI-suggested markdown %, and projected units cleared at that price. E.g. "Spinach 12 bunches · Expires 6pm · Mark down 30% → likely clears by 4pm".
Her Action
Reviews the list (takes 2 min). Approves markdown % per item or adjusts. Taps confirm → Roz notifies Suresh (floor staff) via a simple WhatsApp message with the items and new prices to label. POS price updated automatically.
Outcome
Items marked down at 9am instead of 4pm → 7 more hours to sell. Fewer write-offs. Recovery rate tracked and fed back into next day's markdown model. Over time, markdown % recommendations improve per-SKU.
Edge cases handled
🏷 Batch data missing: "Expiry date not found for 2 items — please check manually" with item names highlighted
✅ Already cleared: if item sells out before Radhika acts, alert auto-dismisses
📉 Deep markdown needed: if item won't sell even at 50% off, Roz flags "Consider donation or write-off" to reduce guilt of discarding

Edge cases handled

👥 No staff available: "All staff already assigned — no one available to move. Consider calling backup?"

P2 · Next
→ Alert layer
Real-Time Stockout Alert
From "customer tells Radhika shelf is empty" → "Roz alerts before the shelf empties"
Trigger
Roz monitors inventory levels continuously. When an SKU drops below a reorder threshold (calculated from average daily sales velocity × lead time from DC), and no DC replenishment is in transit, alert fires. Threshold is store-specific and auto-calibrates weekly.
What Radhika Sees
Alert card: "⚠ Atta (Fortune 5kg) — stock for ~2 hrs left. DC delivery not scheduled. Chase DC?" with one-tap "Call DC" that dials the DC coordinator number directly.
Her Action
Taps "Call DC" or "Transfer from stockroom" (if backup stock exists). If she does nothing, Roz auto-escalates to Vikram (Area Manager) after 30 min — so Radhika is incentivised to act quickly.
Outcome
Stockouts addressed before shelf empties. Lost-sales events reduced. Every stockout event is logged — patterns (e.g. Fortune Atta always runs out on Sundays) feed back into UC-01 order recommendations.
Edge cases handled
📦 Stockroom backup found: Roz checks stockroom inventory first — "6 units in stockroom. Ask Suresh to refill shelf?"
🔁 Repeat stockout: if same SKU stocks out 3× in 2 weeks, Roz flags it in next week's order recommendation with higher buffer qty

Shelf Photo Audit (Planogram Compliance)
Floor staff take a phone photo of a shelf → AI scores it against the planogram in 60 seconds
Trigger
Radhika (or Suresh) opens Roz → "Shelf Check" → points camera at a shelf section. Roz captures the image and sends to the CV model (Infilect InfiViz-style) which identifies SKUs, checks against planogram, and flags gaps or misplacements.
What Radhika Sees
Results in ~60 seconds: "Shelf score: 78/100. Issues: 3 facings of Maggi missing, Promo display not set up in zone 2". Each issue is a tap to dismiss (if fixed) or assign to staff.
Her Action
Reviews issues. Taps to assign to Suresh. Suresh gets a WhatsApp: "Maggi facing in row 3 — please refill". Once done, Suresh taps confirm in Roz. Compliance score updates in Vikram's area-level view.
Outcome
Planogram compliance improves without Vikram having to visit. Promo execution tracked digitally. Over time, AI model accuracy improves with each photo — learns this specific store's layout and SKU set.
Edge cases / constraints
📶 Requires data connection to process image — show "queued" state if offline
💡 Lighting: poor store lighting can reduce accuracy — prompt user to turn on flash
🏗 India-specific: cluttered shelf layouts and high SKU count reduce accuracy vs Western supermarkets — build in "uncertain" state rather than wrong confidence
UC-10
P3 · Later
→ IoT / Cold Chain
Refrigeration Temperature Alert
Cold chain failure is invisible until damage is done — product ruined, wastage spikes, food safety risk
Trigger
IoT temperature sensors on refrigeration units send readings every 5 minutes. If any unit exceeds safe threshold (>4°C for dairy, >-18°C for frozen) for more than 15 consecutive minutes, Roz fires an alert — even at 3am.
What Radhika Sees
High-priority push: "🚨 Dairy fridge 2 — temperature 8°C (safe: <4°C). Action needed now." Alert includes: current temp, duration above threshold, estimated time to unsafe zone, and emergency contact for the refrigeration vendor.
Her Action
One-tap to call the maintenance vendor. One-tap to flag the unit for a technician visit tomorrow if it self-corrected. If she's not available (e.g. off-shift), alert auto-escalates to Vikram after 30 minutes.
Outcome
Cold chain failures caught in minutes vs discovered at opening. Product loss prevented or minimised. Temperature logs stored automatically for food safety compliance audits. Predictive maintenance: recurring patterns flagged before failure ("Unit 2 has had 4 temp spikes this month — service recommended").
Edge cases / constraints
💰 Cost: IoT sensors require upfront hardware investment — start with 1–2 critical units (dairy, frozen) not all fridges
📡 Connectivity: sensors need local gateway device to buffer readings if internet drops
🔔 Alert fatigue: minor temp fluctuations (door left open) should not fire alerts — use sustained-threshold logic, not single-reading spikes
